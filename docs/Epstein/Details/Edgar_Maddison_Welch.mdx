## Assessment: MODERATE SUSPICION

Welch was shot by Kannapolis, NC police after reportedly pulling a handgun during an arrest on an outstanding felony probation violation warrant. The shooting itself has a straightforward law enforcement explanation. However, the timing — weeks before new Epstein file releases reignited public interest in the same elite trafficking claims that motivated Welch's 2016 actions — and his status as the most prominent living symbol of the Pizzagate narrative raise questions about the broader circumstances.

## Circumstances of Death

On the evening of Saturday, January 4, 2025, a Kannapolis Police Department officer on patrol recognized a gray 2001 GMC Yukon as belonging to someone with an outstanding arrest warrant. The officer initiated a traffic stop on North Cannon Boulevard. Welch was the front-seat passenger — not the driver.

According to the Kannapolis Police Department, when an officer opened the front passenger door to arrest Welch on a felony probation violation warrant, Welch pulled a handgun from his jacket and pointed it at the officer. Officers Brooks Jones and Caleb Tate gave repeated commands for Welch to drop the weapon. When Welch reportedly refused, both officers fired their duty weapons, striking Welch. A third officer on scene did not fire.

Welch was transported to Atrium Cabarrus hospital and later transferred to Atrium Health in Charlotte, where he died on January 6, 2025 — two days after the shooting. The driver of the vehicle and a backseat passenger were uninjured.

Kannapolis Police Chief Terry L. Spry released the information on January 9, 2025. The North Carolina State Bureau of Investigation (SBI) was called to conduct an independent investigation, which is standard protocol for officer-involved shootings in North Carolina. Both officers who fired were placed on administrative leave.

## Background

Edgar Maddison Welch grew up in Salisbury, North Carolina. His father, Harry Welch Jr., ran a no-kill dog rescue, and his mother, Terri, was a registered nurse and volunteer firefighter. An older brother died in a car accident in 1996. Welch had two young daughters.

Welch had worked as a warehouse associate and EMT, and had minor acting roles in two independent films. He volunteered in Haiti after the 2010 earthquake. His prior criminal record included a misdemeanor drug possession plea (2007) and DUI charges.

### The Comet Ping Pong Incident (December 4, 2016)

On December 4, 2016, Welch drove approximately 350 miles from Salisbury, NC to Washington, DC, armed with an AR-15 rifle, a .38-caliber revolver, and a shotgun. He entered Comet Ping Pong restaurant — a center of the "Pizzagate" conspiracy theory that falsely claimed Democratic officials ran a child trafficking ring from the restaurant's basement. The restaurant has no basement.

Before entering, he recorded a video message to his daughters: "I can't let you grow up in a world that's so corrupt by evil, without at least standing up for you and for other children just like you."

Welch fired three shots from the AR-15 inside the restaurant, striking walls and a door. Employees and customers (including children) fled. No one was injured. He surrendered to police after they surrounded the building.

### Trial and Sentencing

On March 24, 2017, Welch pleaded guilty to federal interstate transport of firearms and a DC assault with a dangerous weapon charge. On June 22, 2017, U.S. District Judge Ketanji Brown Jackson — five years before her Supreme Court confirmation — sentenced him to four years in federal prison.

In court, Welch said: "Words cannot undo what's already happened." He turned to restaurant employees: "I want to make sure the victims understand how sorry I am."

Judge Jackson described his recklessness as "breathtaking" and said his actions "literally left psychological wreckage."

Welch was transferred to a halfway house on March 3, 2020, and released on May 28, 2020. He was on supervised release at the time of his death, with an outstanding felony probation violation warrant.
